## Setting the Table: What Happened in 2025 (60-Second Recap)

The Big Ten owns college football right now — three straight national titles by three different programs (Michigan '23, Ohio State '24, Indiana '25), each built a completely different way. And the 2025 version was the most preposterous of all: **Indiana went 16-0**, finished No. 1 in SP+, won the Big Ten title game 13-10 over Ohio State, beat Oregon twice (regular season and the Peach Bowl semifinal), and then beat Miami in the national championship game — with Fernando Mendoza going No. 1 overall in the draft afterward. The losingest program in major college football history, two years removed from 9-27 across three seasons, is the defending national champion. Nothing in the sport's history resembles it.

The league was violently top-heavy: per Connelly, the Big Ten had three teams rated better than anyone in the SEC and seven rated worse than anyone in the SEC. Ohio State went 12-0 in the regular season playing the slowest tempo in the country, then scored 24 combined points in the CCG loss to Indiana and a Cotton Bowl quarterfinal loss to Miami — your Canes' scalp, and the game that lit the "Ryan Day is too conservative" discourse on fire. Oregon went 13-2 with both losses to Indiana and is now 1-5 against eventual title-game teams and 37-0 against everyone else over three years.

Below the summit, chaos: Penn State fired James Franklin mid-season after three straight one-score losses (including a 42-37 stunner to an 0-4 UCLA team). And in December, Michigan fired Sherrone Moore for cause after an investigation found an inappropriate relationship with a staffer; he was arrested days later and faces felony and misdemeanor charges. Michigan pursued Kalen DeBoer, didn't get him, and landed Kyle Whittingham — who had just announced his retirement after 21 seasons at Utah — on a five-year deal the day after Christmas.

**The 2025 ledger:** Indiana 16-0, Oregon 13-2, Ohio State 12-2, Michigan 9-4, USC 9-4, Iowa 9-4, Washington 9-4, Illinois 9-4, Minnesota 8-5, Nebraska 7-6, Northwestern 7-6, Penn State 7-6, Rutgers 5-7, Wisconsin 4-8, Maryland 4-8, Michigan State 4-8, UCLA 3-9, Purdue 2-10.

**What's new for 2026:**
- **Four new head coaches, three of them shockers:** Kyle Whittingham (Michigan), Matt Campbell (Penn State, from Iowa State — bringing roughly two dozen Cyclones with him), Bob Chesney (UCLA, from James Madison), and Pat Fitzgerald (Michigan State — a hire Connelly openly panned). Wisconsin went the other way: it publicly retained Luke Fickell in November with a bigger NIL commitment, then won its next two home games.
- **A coordinator carousel for the ages:** Arthur Smith (NFL) now runs Ohio State's offense; Gary Patterson runs USC's defense; Chip Kelly is Northwestern's OC; D'Anton Lynn left USC for Penn State; Jim Knowles left Penn State for Tennessee; Oregon lost both coordinators to head jobs (Will Stein to Kentucky, Tosh Lupoi to Cal) and promoted from within.
- **The champs reloaded scarier than anyone:** Cignetti's portal class might be better than the one that won the title — headlined by TCU's Josh Hoover, statistically more proven than Mendoza was a year ago.

## The Playoff Picture

- **SP+:** Ohio State (41.4%) and Oregon (40.0%) both have roughly two-in-five odds of 11+ wins; Indiana is at 32.8%. Nobody else is above Penn State's 13.6% — and PSU's number is inflated by the softest contender schedule in the league (only three projected top-40 Big Ten opponents, while Washington plays six and Michigan/USC/Iowa play five each).
- **CBS (Crawford):** Oregon 11-1 (lone loss at Ohio State in November), then wins the CCG rematch in Indianapolis — Ducks champs, Buckeyes in as an at-large again, Indiana comfortably in the field. That's three near-lock bids with USC/Michigan/Washington/Iowa fighting for a possible fourth.
- **Vegas:** The national board says it plainly — Ohio State favored to win it all, Indiana third, Oregon lurking. The Big Ten is priced as the sport's center of gravity for a fourth straight year.
- **The structural note:** the Big Three barely play each other's supporting casts. Oct. 17 (OSU at Indiana) and Nov. 7 (Oregon at Ohio State) are the only two head-to-heads among them in the regular season — Oregon and Indiana don't meet until Indianapolis, if at all.

## Sidebar: The Miami Threads (Work These Into Every Segment)

Your show has skin in this conference's story, so lean in:

1. **Miami is the reason Ryan Day is on notice.** The Canes' 14-0 halftime lead and Cotton Bowl quarterfinal win — against a Buckeye offense so slow it managed three real second-half possessions — is the game every Ohio State talking head cites. When OSU hired Arthur Smith to modernize the offense, that was, functionally, a response to Miami.
2. **Indiana beat Miami for the title with the Canes leading inside the final minute.** Every Indiana 2026 conversation is a rematch conversation for your audience. Josh Hoover replacing Mendoza is the single most important QB change in the sport from Miami's what-if perspective.
3. **Mendoza went No. 1 overall.** A Cal transfer who beat Miami for a ring — and Miami now employs the portal QB (Darian Mensah) everyone will compare him to all season.
4. **Penn State 1986 anniversary alert:** Connelly's anniversary section spotlights Penn State beating Miami in the Fiesta Bowl for the 1986 title — the Testaverde five-pick game — which turns 40 this season. Expect it everywhere; have your counter-programming ready.

# TIER 1 — THE BIG THREE

## Ohio State Buckeyes

**2025:** 12-2 — a 12-0 regular season (including 14-7 over Texas in the opener), then 24 combined points in the CCG loss to Indiana and the Cotton Bowl loss to Miami. **SP+:** #1 overall, #1 defense, #2 offense, 10.2 avg wins — with the No. 4 schedule. **CBS:** 11-1, loses the Indy rematch to Oregon, makes the CFP at-large. **Vegas:** 9.5 total, +185 conference, +600 national favorite.

**1) The roster.** Julian Sayin returns after a ruthlessly efficient first year (first nationally in success rate, trained-wheels version), and so does the best player in college football: Jeremiah Smith, entering his last dance with 2,558 yards and 29 TDs in two seasons. Sophomore RB Bo Jackson emerged as a yards-after-contact monster; three O-line starters return, including All-Big Ten pair Luke Montgomery and Austin Siereveld. The receiver depth behind Smith is the offensive question — only Jackson and Brandon Inniss were targeted more than five times among returnees — answered with transfers Devin McCuin (UTSA) and Kyle Parker (LSU) plus five-star freshman Chris Henry Jr. The flip side is a defensive exodus: only two of the nine every-game starters return (S Jaylen McClain, CB Jermaine Mathews Jr.), with three defenders drafted in the first 11 picks. Day signed eight defensive transfers — Alabama's Qua Russaw and James Smith up front, Duke's Terry Moore (a Connelly favorite) and FSU's Earl Little Jr. behind — and will lean on a sophomore wave: Zion Grady, Riley Pettijohn, Devin Sanchez, Leroy Roker III. New OC: Arthur Smith, seven years an NFL coordinator/head coach. Matt Patricia returns for Year 2 running the defense. Watch list: Smith (obviously), Jackson, Sanchez, and whichever WR2 emerges.

**2) Identity, strengths, weaknesses, goals.** Offense: last year it was slow-tempo, short-throw efficiency ball — first in success rate, last in urgency, and it died in the two games that mattered (the fourth-quarter punting drive against Miami will live in Columbus infamy). Arthur Smith's history says run-game excellence plus safe horizontal passing — which is either the perfect maturation of Sayin or more of the same disease with an NFL accent. Defense: back-to-back No. 1 in SP+ under Patricia, now facing a genuine transition year. Strengths: the QB-WR1 duo, O-line, recruiting conveyor, and the projection systems' unanimous belief. Weaknesses: tempo/urgency in big games, defensive continuity, WR depth, and a schedule that removes cruise control — at Texas Week 2, at Indiana, at USC, at Iowa, Oregon in November. Season goals: explicitly, the Big Ten title Day hasn't won since 2020, and a January where the offense attacks instead of protects. Connelly's sharpest point: the harder schedule and defensive turnover might *force* the urgency that's been missing, and that could make them better in December.

**3) Prediction.** 11-1 with the loss coming in one of the road gauntlet games, a CCG berth, and a CFP run that finally answers the Day question one way or the other. I'd take them over Oregon in Indy — but note that Crawford wouldn't, and the 13-10 CCG loss plus the Miami game means Day enters the year 0-for-his-last-2 in games decided by his own aggression. The stakes for your show: if Ohio State wins it all, the Miami quarterfinal becomes the sport's favorite "that's what woke them up" story.

## Oregon Ducks

**2025:** 13-2 (8-1) — both losses to Indiana (regular season and the Peach Bowl semifinal). Now 1-5 against eventual title-game teams over three years and 37-0 against everybody else. **SP+:** #2, 10.2 avg wins, #1 offense. **CBS:** 11-1 (lone loss at Ohio State), then beats OSU in the Indy rematch — Big Ten champs. **Vegas:** 10.5 total (over is popular at +116), +270.

**1) The roster.** The most important returnee in the sport: QB Dante Moore, a projected top-10 pick who came back after 3,565 yards and 30 TDs, and he brought his whole defensive senior class with him — Matayo Uiagalelei, Bear Alexander, A'Mauri Washington, and Teitum Tuioti all passed on the NFL. Oregon leads the nation with 200 returning starts. The sophomore skill wave is absurd: RBs Jordon Davison and Dierre Hill Jr. (1,323 combined yards at 7.0 a carry, 20 TDs), WRs Dakorien Moore and Jeremiah McClellan, plus a healthy Evan Stewart and transfers Iverson Hooks and Simeon Price. The secondary might have the country's highest upside — sophomores Brandon Finney Jr. and Aaron Flowers plus transfers Koi Perich (poached from Minnesota — an intraconference heist Connelly loves) and Carl Williams IV (Baylor). The two cracks: both coordinators left for head jobs (Stein to Kentucky, Lupoi to Cal), replaced by internal promotions Drew Mehringer (OC) and Chris Hampton (DC); and the O-line is rebuilt for a second straight year around All-American center Iapani Laloulu, with a Yale transfer (Michael Bennett III) and a sophomore (Fox Crader) candidates at tackle. Watch list: Moore (Heisman shortlist), Dakorien Moore, Uiagalelei, Perich.

**2) Identity, strengths, weaknesses, goals.** Offense: No. 1 in projected offensive SP+ — spacing, tempo variation, and the deepest skill room in the country. Defense: a big, ridiculously experienced front six with a ball-hawking back end — Connelly says he'd take Oregon's defensive personnel over almost anyone's. Strengths: continuity, two-deep talent, coaching stability at the top (Lanning is 48-8). Weaknesses: the O-line has been physically overwhelmed in each of the last two CFP exits — that is THE program-defining flaw — plus first-year coordinators in the biggest games. Season goals: unspoken but universal — stop being the best team that can't beat the final boss. Trips to USC (Week 4) and Ohio State (Nov. 7) are the season's proof points; Indiana looms only in Indy.

**3) Prediction.** 11-1, Big Ten champions (I'll ride with Crawford here — the roster balance argument is real), a top-2 seed, and the "is it finally Oregon's turn" question answered in January rather than November. If the tackles hold up against elite fronts, this is the most complete team in the country. If they don't, it's 37-0-against-everyone-else, part four.

## Indiana Hoosiers

**2025:** 16-0. National champions. First in SP+. Beat Ohio State in Indy, beat Oregon twice, beat Miami for the title. **2026:** Cignetti is 27-2 in Bloomington. **SP+:** #5, 9.9 avg wins (7.0 conference). **Vegas:** 10.5 total, +260.

**1) The roster.** Gone: Mendoza (No. 1 overall pick), two 1,000-yard rushers, two 800-yard receivers, eight draft picks. Cignetti's answer was a portal class Connelly likes *better* than last year's — a sentence he calls scary. QB Josh Hoover (TCU) arrives with 9,629 career passing yards and 71 TDs — more proven than Mendoza was at the same point. RB Turbo Richard, WRs Nick Marsh (Michigan State — scouts' darling) and Shazz Preston (Tulane's top big-play threat on a CFP team), Wisconsin's best lineman (Joe Brunner), three transfer edges with elite pressure rates (Chiddi Obiazor, Tobi Osunsanmi, Josh Burnham), and FCS-champion corner Carson Williams. The internal star: WR Charlie Becker, who averaged an outrageous 3.7 yards per route over the season's final two months — better than Jeremiah Smith's full-season number — with game-winning catches in IU's three tightest wins. The trenches keep All-American LT Carter Smith, guards Bray Lynch and Drew Evans, DTs Tyrique Tucker and Mario Landino, and semifinal breakout DE Daniel Ndukwe. Watch list: Hoover (interception habit is the one red flag — Cignetti has already had to publicly defend him), Becker, the Becker/Marsh/Preston trio.

**2) Identity, strengths, weaknesses, goals.** Offense: Mike Shanahan's precision spread — the scheme made a Heisman winner out of a Cal transfer; now it gets a more experienced arm. Defense: Bryant Haines' aggressive structure returns proven interior anchors but needs the transfer edges to hit, because line play has powered this entire two-year run. Strengths: the best coaching staff in the sport by results, scouting-and-development edge, portal precision, house-money-turned-empire culture. Weaknesses: 52nd in returning production (normal for champions, abnormal for Indiana), Hoover's turnovers, and the target on their back — every opponent's Super Bowl now. Season goals: prove it's a program, not a comet — the exact bet Connelly makes by refusing to project them below the top 10.

**3) Prediction.** 10-2, a third straight CFP, and no repeat — the October gauntlet (at Ohio State's building flipped: OSU comes to Bloomington Oct. 17, then at Michigan Oct. 24, at Washington Nov. 21) takes one bite, and the title-defense tax takes another. But betting against Cignetti has been the worst trade in college football for two years, and if Hoover cuts the picks, all bets are off. For your listeners: this is the revenge tour's final boss, and the rematch can only happen in the CFP.

# TIER 2 — THE CHASE PACK

## USC Trojans

**2025:** 9-4 (7-2), 16th in SP+ — the offense (9th) carried a 36th-ranked defense. **SP+:** #13, 7.8 avg wins, No. 11 schedule. **Vegas:** +1500.

**1) The roster.** QB Jayden Maiava returns off a 3,711-yard, 24-TD season with the best QBR of any returning Big Ten starter — virtually unsackable, a perfectly timed scrambler, a point guard. His problem: seven of the top eight receivers are gone. Sophomore Tanook Hines is the star-in-waiting, NC State transfer Terrell Anderson (16.1 per catch, 73.6% catch rate) is the safety valve, and a wave of blue-chip freshmen (Boobie Feaster, Trent Mosley, Mark Bowman) fills in. The backfield is a strength — King Miller (4.2 yards after contact as a walk-on freshman) and Waymond Jordan, plus three returning line starters. The story is the defense: coordinator D'Anton Lynn left for Penn State, and Lincoln Riley replaced him with 66-year-old Gary Patterson, whose last top-20 unit was 2014. Nine of 16 rotation defenders return, most of them former blue-chip underclassmen — Kameryn Crawford, Jahkeem Stewart, Jadyn Walker, Marcelles Williams — plus veteran transfers (Zuriah Fisher, Alex VanSumeren, Deven Bryant) and mega-freshmen (Luke Wafle, Tomuhini Topui). Watch list: Maiava, Hines, Stewart.

**2) Identity, strengths, weaknesses, goals.** Offense: Riley's spacing game with a genuinely elite QB — top-6 projected again. Defense: Patterson's 4-2-5 was the most influential scheme of the 2000s; the question is whether the innovator still has an edge now that everyone runs his ideas. Strengths: QB, backfield, recruiting surge, and finally some roster continuity (65% returning production). Weaknesses: receiver proof, the Patterson bet, and a schedule with five projected top-40 league opponents — including Oregon AND Washington at home back-to-back before the Oct. 10 trip to Penn State. Season goals: the CFP, full stop — Riley's fifth year, most balanced roster, no more excuses.

**3) Prediction.** 9-3 and the bubble's loudest argument. If Patterson's unit is merely top-30, USC wins ten and gets in; Connelly calls them the conference's most fascinating team for exactly that reason. Riley's seat isn't hot, but a fourth straight season outside the CFP with this roster would start the countdown clock in earnest.

## Michigan Wolverines

**2025:** 9-4 (7-2), 25th in SP+ — then the December implosion: Sherrone Moore fired for cause Dec. 10, arrested and charged days later. **2026 coach:** Kyle Whittingham, first year — 21 seasons and a school-record 177 wins at Utah, hired Dec. 26 after Michigan's DeBoer pursuit fizzled. **SP+:** #14, 7.5 avg wins, No. 5 schedule. **Vegas:** +1500.

**1) The roster.** The crown jewel stayed: Bryce Underwood, the No. 1 recruit in the 2025 class, enters Year 2 after a freshman season of flashes (506 non-sack rushing yards on just 68 carries). Whittingham imported his Utah operating system wholesale — OC Jason Beck, who built a top-6 offense around dual-threat Devon Dampier last year, DC Jay Hill (from BYU via Utah lineage), O-line coach Jim Harding, and three Utah players: star DE John Henry Daley (a Connelly top-10 transfer, racing back from an October Achilles rupture), DT Jonah Lea'ea, and do-everything nickel Smith Snowden. Returning defensive core: DT Trey Pierce, LB Troy Bowles, CBs Jyaire Hill and Zeke Berry. The skill room is young: sophomore WR Andrew Marsh and RB Jordan Marshall are budding stars; transfers JJ Buchanan and Jaime Ffrench Jr. plus freshman Salesi Moa fill out a thin pass-catching group. Watch list: Underwood (the whole enterprise), Daley's Achilles, Marsh.

**2) Identity, strengths, weaknesses, goals.** Offense: Beck's efficiency-first, QB-run-leveraged system — the fascinating question is whether he runs Underwood like he ran Dampier (who carried it twice as often) or builds something more conventional around a five-star arm. Defense: Hill's aggressive Utah/BYU structure with more raw talent than he's ever coached. Strengths: Underwood's ceiling, coaching pedigree, culture reset after a traumatic winter. Weaknesses: Connelly's two big flags — can a 66-year-old built on chip-on-the-shoulder rosters connect with blue-chippers, and does the Beck-Underwood marriage actually fit — plus receiver depth and the league's No. 5 schedule (Indiana visits Oct. 24; the Ohio State finale is in Columbus). Season goals: stability first, 9 wins second, and a healthy Underwood entering 2027 as the Heisman favorite.

**3) Prediction.** 8-4, competitive in everything, with the Underwood leap arriving in bursts rather than in full. The Whittingham hire is either a masterstroke bridge or an awkward era mismatch, and we genuinely won't know until October. No seat, obviously — but the administration that botched December needs this to look functional fast.

## Penn State Nittany Lions

**2025:** 7-6 (3-6) — Franklin fired mid-season after three straight one-score losses; the roster still finished 15th in SP+. **2026 coach:** Matt Campbell, first year — the Iowa State program-builder, arriving with roughly 24 former Cyclones including 11-13 projected starters. **SP+:** #17, 9.1 avg wins... on the No. 59 schedule. **Vegas:** 9.5 total, +2000.

**1) The roster.** The most extreme turnover experiment among contenders: only seven of 35 players with 200+ snaps return, three of them starters (plus returning-from-injury star LB Tony Rojas). The offense is essentially Iowa State East — QB Rocco Becht, RB Carson Hansen, WRs Chase Sowell and Brett Eskildsen, TE Benjamin Brahmer, two Cyclone guards — plus Ohio State RB transfer James Peoples and returning TE Andrew Rappleyea and G Anthony Donkoh. The defense is stronger: Rojas, nickel Zion Tracy, and CB Audavion Collins lead the holdovers; Campbell's Cyclone imports (LBs Kooper Ebel and Caleb Bacon, CB Jeremiah Cooper, safeties Marcus Neal Jr. — a Connelly top-10 transfer — and Jamison Patton) merge with four 319-pound-plus transfer tackles; and new DC D'Anton Lynn (from USC) inherits a better culture than he left. Watch list: Becht, Neal, Rojas.

**2) Identity, strengths, weaknesses, goals.** Offense: Campbell ball — physical, patient, low-variance... and drawn from an ISU offense that ranked just 63rd. Defense: Lynn's multiple structure with real talent; this should be top-25 quickly. Strengths: coaching floor, special teams (6th projected), and a schedule gift — only three projected top-40 Big Ten opponents, no Indiana, no Ohio State, no Oregon. Weaknesses: the whole premise — Connelly's warning is that mass transfers usually flow FROM better teams, not from a 33rd-ranked one to a program that hasn't ranked that low since 2015. And the standard: Franklin averaged top-11 finishes; "Iowa State's best" was one top-20 SP+ team in a decade. Season goals: 9-plus wins, a CFP bubble seat, and a culture Campbell can actually recruit to.

**3) Prediction.** 9-3, right on the CBS number, with the USC visit on Oct. 10 as the season's fork: win it and PSU is a legitimate dark horse (SP+ gives them 13.6% at 11+ wins, fourth in the league); lose it and the schedule-inflation critics take a bow. The strangest range of outcomes of any top-25 team — borderline top-15 and outside the top 40 are both live, per Connelly himself.

## Washington Huskies

**2025:** 9-4 (5-4) — but 0-4 against the four best defenses they played (9.3 PPG in those games), 45.1 PPG against everyone else. **SP+:** #21, 7.8 avg wins. **Vegas:** +3000.

**1) The roster.** QB Demond Williams Jr.'s offseason was a saga — signed, briefly left for the portal, came back calling the departure a mistake — but he's the engine: 7.5 yards per dropback, 7.3 per carry, massive upside, too many sacks. Four line starters return plus Sam Houston transfer Kolt Dieterich; the skill room lost leading rusher Jonah Coleman and WR Denzel Boston, replaced by sophomores (Jordan Washington, Dezmen Roebuck, Chris Lawson) and vets Jayden Limar (Oregon) and Christian Moss. Ryan Walters' defense surged 36th→9th in Year 1 and returns keys at every level (Elinneus Davis, Jacob Lane, Xe'ree Alexander, Alex McLaughlin, three sophomore DBs) — but Davis is the only returning tackle of six who logged 100+ snaps, so transfers Kai McClendon, Darin Conley, and DeSean Watts must hit. Watch list: Williams, Roebuck, Lane.

**2) Identity, strengths, weaknesses, goals.** Fisch's pro-style with a jet-fueled QB; Walters' aggressive defense. Strength: everything against normal teams. Weakness: everything against great defenses — and the 2026 slate serves Iowa, Penn State, Indiana (Nov. 21, Connelly's sneaky-huge game), and Oregon. Goals: solve one elite defense and the CFP conversation opens.

**3) Prediction.** 8-4 with a genuine upset in the bag — this profile (blowout artist vs. the middle class, stuck vs. the elite) usually breaks through exactly once a year. Fisch is building something real; Year 3 tells us the ceiling.

# TIER 3 — BOWL LOCKS WITH UPSIDE

## Iowa Hawkeyes

**2025:** 9-4 (6-3), 12th in SP+ with the No. 6 defense — again. **SP+:** #22, 8.0 avg wins. **2026:** Ferentz Year 28.

**The gist.** The most stable program in America faces its least stable roster: dead last in the Big Ten in returning production (104th nationally), with only six of 17 rotation defenders back and all four top TFL producers gone. Phil Parker hasn't ranked below sixth in defensive SP+ since 2018, so he gets the benefit of the doubt, but Ferentz signed eight defensive transfers — a franchise record by volume — many from the FCS ranks (Kahmari Brown, Anthony Hawkins, Xavier Styles) around holdovers Bryce Hawthorne, Jayden Montgomery, and Zach Lutmer. The offense is quietly the steadier unit under Tim Lester: RBs Kamari Moulton and Xavier Williams return, FCS star LJ Phillips Jr. (2,100+ scrimmage yards at South Dakota, a Connelly favorite) arrives, All-America G Kade Pieper anchors the line, and sophomore TE DJ Vonnahme was the most efficient per-route tight end in the country. The QB derby (Hank Brown vs. Jeremy Hecklinski) needs to clear only the modest bar Mark Gronowski left. **Prediction:** 8-4. Iowa is the sport's most reliable 8-4, and the Oct. trip by Ohio State to Kinnick is the annual rock-fight-of-the-year candidate. Ferentz's seat: geological.

## Illinois Fighting Illini

**2025:** 9-4, powered by the FBS-best 13 one-score wins over three years (.765 win rate in them). **SP+:** #32, 7.0 avg wins.

**The gist.** The close-game machine loses its trigger: Luke Altmyer's three-year run ends, replaced by East Carolina transfer Katin Houser (13-6 as a starter, statistically Altmyer-like, fewer sacks, less scramble juice). The receiving corps refreshes with FIU's Alex Perry and FAU's Jayshon Platt around Hudson Clement; all three lead backs return (Kaden Feagin now at TE); but only one O-line starter is back. The defense is the bigger renovation — Montana legend Bobby Hauck takes over as coordinator with only four of 15 front-six regulars returning; the secondary (Xavier Scott back healthy, Matthew Bailey, Tanner Heckel) is the strength. Bielema's confidence is a weapon, but Connelly doubts the front-six personnel. **Prediction:** 7-5 — the one-score luck regresses at the exact moment the roster resets. Bielema's seat is cool; the program's floor is his legacy.

## Nebraska Cornhuskers

**2025:** 7-6 — Year 3 stagnation instead of the classic Rhule Year 3 leap. **SP+:** #37, 6.0 avg wins, No. 12 schedule.

**The gist.** Dylan Raiola is gone; UNLV's Anthony Colandrea arrives — a scramble-heavy, big-play-hunting, occasionally self-destructive joyride (nine career multi-INT games) that Dana Holgorsen will point at WRs Nyziah Hunter and Jacory Barney Jr. The line adds ISU, LSU, and South Carolina transfers; sophomore RBs Mekhi Nelson and Isaiah Mozee replace 1,400-yard Emmett Johnson — with the July complication that Nelson was just arrested on an aggravated battery charge, status unresolved. Defense: Rob Aurich is the fourth coordinator in five years, importing his San Diego State fix-it kit plus SDSU players around promising sophomores (Williams Nwaneri, Kade Pietrzak). **Prediction:** 6-6 or 7-5 against the league's 12th-toughest schedule, and either way the Rhule conversation sharpens: two straight 7-6s reads as progress until it reads as ceiling. Warm-adjacent, trending on results.

## Northwestern Wildcats

**2025:** 7-6, best team in five years, top-30 defense and special teams. **SP+:** #49, 5.4 avg wins... with the No. 6 schedule in America.

**The gist.** The vision is delicious — Chip Kelly as OC, MSU transfer Aidan Chiles at QB, and a roster built on sheer mass (three transfer linemen averaging 6-7, 328; a 225-pound RB; a 275-pound TE; a Division II tight end sensation in Luke Dehnicke) — and the schedule is a war crime: road games at ALL THREE of Ohio State, Oregon, and Indiana, plus Penn State, Iowa, and Illinois at home. The new lakefront-replacing Ryan Field opens in October. Six of 15 defensive regulars return, all six good (Fussell, Fitzgerald, Turner headline). **Prediction:** 5-7 with one upset that breaks somebody's season — Connelly's betting the same. Braun's seat is cool; the athletic department just built him a stadium.

# TIER 4 — THE MUDDLED MIDDLE

## Minnesota Golden Gophers
**2025:** 8-5. **SP+:** #45, 6.0 wins. Fleck's Year 10 formula is unchanged: top-25 defense (five in six years projected), physical ball, and an offense that keeps the ceiling nailed shut — no top-50 unit since 2020, and 14 or fewer points in 22 of his 44 losses. QB Drake Lindsey returns for Year 2 with experience everywhere (Darius Taylor, three linemen, Cincinnati transfer Noah Jennings), but the numbers were grim (62.0 QBR, 9.7 yards per completion). The defense lost its best player to a conference rival — safety Koi Perich to Oregon, the pettiest transfer of the Big Ten cycle — but returns Anthony Smith (12.5 sacks), Maverick Baranowski, and John Nestor (six INTs). **Prediction:** 6-6, a bowl, and the same conversation next July. Fleck's seat: room temperature forever.

## UCLA Bruins
**2025:** 3-9 — an 0-4 start, Foster fired after three games, a shock three-game surge (ending the Franklin era at Penn State 42-37 and blowing out MSU), then five straight blowout losses and the program's worst SP+ finish since 1963. **2026:** Bob Chesney arrives from James Madison with 132 career wins across three levels and half the Dukes' roster — RB Wayne Knight (1,770 scrimmage yards), DE Sahir West (16 TFLs), All-Sun Belt linemen Riley Robell and Carter Sweazie, and more. The headline retention: QB Nico Iamaleava stayed, alongside G Eugene Brooks and CB Rodrick Pleasant. **SP+:** #48, 6.0 wins. Connelly won't predict a Cignetti-style surge but openly likes the setup: proven production in nearly every unit, from a coach who has won everywhere. **Prediction:** 6-6 and bowling, with Nico's redemption arc the storyline — vibes can change fast, and this program just proved it in both directions within one season.

## Maryland Terrapins 🔥
**2025:** 4-8, second straight. **SP+:** #53, 5.8 wins — but No. 2 NATIONALLY in returning production. This is the all-in year Locksley designed: he handed blue-chip freshman QB Malik Washington the keys in 2025, ate the losses, kept him out of the portal, and returns 13 of 19 defensive regulars including a monster sophomore edge duo (Sidney Stewart + Zahir Mathis: 20.5 TFLs as freshmen). Washington's flaws were real (15th of 17 B1G QBs in QBR, heat-seeking fastballs off receivers' hands) but so was the sack avoidance and scrambling; new OC Clint Trickett and transfer targets (Chris Durr Jr., Na'eem Abdul-Rahim Gladding) are the support package. **Hot seat, full treatment:** Year 8, 37-49, under .500 across any window you pick — the development-year gamble only pays if 2026 produces a bowl and visible Washington growth. **How he escapes:** 6-6+ and a top-40 defense; the roster genuinely supports it. **How it ends:** another 4-8 with this much continuity would remove every argument. **Prediction:** 6-6, bowl, survival by a nose.

## Wisconsin Badgers 🔥
**2025:** 4-8, the worst season since 1990 — and then the school retained Fickell anyway in early November, with more NIL money, and watched the team upset Washington and Illinois immediately after. **SP+:** #62, 5.4 wins. The bet for Year 4: Old Dominion QB Colton Joseph (2,624 pass/1,100 rush/34 TDs), Connelly's second-favorite transfer in the country and a potential "program savior" — his words — behind a retooled backfield (Abu Sama III, Nate Palmer, Darrion Dupree) with a defense (Jung, Catalano, Cheeks, Posa) that has never been the problem. The problem: the offense has been 86th-or-worse in offensive SP+ for three straight years, and only one returning player caught more than 11 passes. **The seat:** publicly cooled, functionally warm — nobody, including Connelly, knows where the Year 5 bar sits, which is its own kind of pressure. **Prediction:** 6-6 and a bowl — Joseph is a genuine difference-maker, and 17-21 coaches who get public votes of confidence tend to get one real bounce. If it's 4-8 again, the NIL-instead-of-buyout decision becomes a case study.

# TIER 5 — JUST LOOKING FOR A PATH TO 6-6

## Rutgers Scarlet Knights 🔥-adjacent
**2025:** 5-7 with the best Schiano offense since 2007 — wasted by a defense that fell to 95th in SP+ (dead last nationally in yards per successful play), which is the single most un-Schiano sentence ever written. **SP+:** #61, 5.4 wins. The response: total defensive teardown — South Dakota's Travis Johansen as coordinator (his first FBS job), only TWO returning defenders with 100+ snaps, and a two-deep of G6/FCS disruptors. The offense keeps the fun: WR KJ Duff (maybe the league's second-best returning receiver), RB Antwan Raymond, and a QB derby between BC transfer Dylan Lonergan and sophomore AJ Surace. **Prediction:** 5-7. Schiano's equity is enormous, but an 18th-year legacy coach betting his identity on an FCS coordinator and 15 new defensive starters is the definition of a prove-it season.

## Michigan State Spartans
**2025:** 4-8; Jonathan Smith fired after going 4-15 across parts of two seasons. **2026:** Pat Fitzgerald, hired the very next day — a move Connelly graded harshly and stands by: a coach who went 4-20 in his last two Northwestern seasons handed a big-time job with no interim proving ground. **SP+:** #67, 4.4 wins. The plan: run the ball (UConn's 1,240-yard Cam Edwards leads four transfer RBs) and steal possessions (transfer DBs Tre Bell, Tyran Chappell, Michael Richard, Devin Vaught combined for 12 INTs). The problems: sophomore Alessio Milivojevic starts at QB mostly by default, the receiver room has no proven playmakers, and depth everywhere is a coin flip. **Prediction:** 4-8. Even Connelly's most generous read is "if Fitzgerald succeeds here, it probably won't be in 2026."

## Purdue Boilermakers
**2025:** 2-10 (0-9). **SP+:** #83, 3.5 wins. Odom's Year 2 after the 54-transfer Year 1: a calmer 29-transfer reload around QB Ryan Browne, Texas RB Jerrick Gibson, and a defense with a few real Big Ten players (Ian Jeffries, Charles Correa, FIU do-everything nickel Mister Clark — a Connelly favorite). The program has a documented history of decade-long walkabouts, and Indiana's simultaneous ascension is salt in every wound. **Prediction:** 3-9, better on tape than in the standings, with the Bucket game as the season.
